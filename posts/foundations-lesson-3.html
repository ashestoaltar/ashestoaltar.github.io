Foundations · Lesson 3

Torah as Covenant Instruction

Few words are more misunderstood in modern Christianity than Torah. For some it means “legalism.” For others it is the “old way” that Messiah came to remove. But within Scripture itself, Torah is a gift—a gracious revelation of how a redeemed people are to live as God’s own.

Torah means instruction

The Hebrew word Torah is often translated “law,” but its basic sense is instruction, teaching, guidance. The God who rescued Israel from Egypt did not leave His people to guess how to live. He spoke. He gave concrete ways of life that reflected His character—justice, mercy, holiness, care for the vulnerable, and distinct worship.

Importantly, Torah is given after redemption. Israel is not told to keep Torah in order to earn rescue. They are rescued by grace, then instructed how to live in covenant faithfulness.

Delight, not mere duty

The Psalms do not speak of Torah as a crushing weight. They speak of it as delight:

“Oh, how I love Your law! It is my meditation all the day.” (Psalm 119:97)

Torah shows Israel what God loves and what He hates. It trains the heart. It shapes a people who embody God’s character in the world. When Israel treats Torah as a checklist, they miss the point. When they ignore it, they break covenant. The faithful response is love-driven obedience.

Common misunderstandings

Many of us have been taught things about Torah that do not match the biblical picture:

  • “Torah was Plan A, Messiah is Plan B.”
    In reality, Messiah is the goal Torah points toward. The story was always heading there.
  • “Torah is about works, the New Testament is about grace.”
    Scripture consistently shows grace before obedience. The Exodus comes before Sinai.
  • “Torah was impossible to keep.”
    While no one keeps it perfectly, the text often speaks of people walking blamelessly or turning back in repentance. Torah anticipates both faithfulness and mercy.

Messiah and Torah

Yeshua does not dismiss Torah. He intensifies and internalizes it. He exposes the heart beneath the commandments and calls His disciples to a righteousness that goes deeper than mere external compliance.

In Him, the prophetic promise is fulfilled that God would write His Torah on the hearts of His people (Jeremiah 31:33). The question is no longer whether Torah matters, but how it is to be lived in light of His life, death, resurrection, and kingship.

Why Torah still matters for us

As disciples, we do not return to Torah as if Messiah never came. But neither do we treat Torah as irrelevant. It reveals the character of the God we claim to follow. It helps us discern what faithfulness looks like in a world that constantly pulls us toward other allegiances.

We learn to ask:

  • What does this command reveal about God’s heart?
  • How is this fulfilled, deepened, or clarified in Messiah?
  • What does it mean for us to walk in the same pattern of holiness and justice today?

In the next lesson we will turn to the prophets, exile, and the hope of restoration—and see how they confront covenant unfaithfulness while holding out a future hope rooted in God’s promises.
