Foundations · Lesson 2

How the Bible Tells Its Story

In Lesson 1 we saw that the Bible is not a random anthology but the covenant story of God and His people. In this lesson we look at how that story is told. The Bible does not simply drop isolated doctrines from heaven. It weaves meaning through narrative, poetry, patterns, and what we often call “typology” – earlier events, people, and symbols that foreshadow greater realities still to come.

Story before system

Modern readers are often trained to think in systems and bullet points. Scripture, by contrast, is overwhelmingly narrative. Genesis, Exodus, Joshua, Judges, Samuel, Kings, the Gospels and Acts all move the story forward. Even the letters and psalms assume we know the story they are commenting on.

This means we do not truly understand a verse until we know the story it belongs to. The covenant is not given as an abstract list of ideas but as God’s actions in history: creating, calling, rescuing, judging, restoring.

Patterns and repetition

One of the key ways Scripture teaches is by repeating patterns. We meet familiar shapes in different generations:

  • A garden or land given, then lost.
  • A younger son chosen over an older.
  • A righteous sufferer vindicated by God.
  • Exile followed by a return and rebuilding.

These repetitions are not accidents. They train us to see how God works, and to recognize when He is doing something similar again in a deeper way. When Yeshua feeds in the wilderness, passes through water, or speaks on a mountain, those are not random details—they echo earlier moments in Israel’s story.

Typology: shadows and substances

The New Testament often speaks of earlier parts of Scripture as “shadows” or “types” pointing toward Messiah. Paul can say that the events in the wilderness “happened to them as examples” and “were written for our admonition” (1 Corinthians 10:11). The writer of Hebrews speaks of the tabernacle as “a copy and shadow of the heavenly things” (Hebrews 8:5).

A type is not a prediction in the narrow sense. It is a pattern, a shape in history that finds its fullest meaning in Messiah. Adam, Israel, David, the temple, the sacrifices – all of these are real in their own right, and at the same time they point beyond themselves.

Reading with the grain of Scripture

When we pay attention to narrative, patterns, and typology, we begin to read with the grain of Scripture instead of against it. Rather than forcing the Bible into our own systems, we let its own storytelling shape our understanding. We ask questions like:

  • How does this story echo earlier stories?
  • What covenant promises or warnings are being picked up here?
  • How might this event be a “shadow” of something greater fulfilled in Messiah?

This does not mean we can make patterns say whatever we want. Patterns are grounded in the text itself, and they are confirmed by how later biblical authors use earlier passages. Our task is to pay attention, to listen, and to be willing to see the connections Scripture itself makes.

Why this matters for discipleship

As disciples, we are not memorizing random facts; we are being formed by a story. Seeing how the Bible tells that story helps us locate ourselves within it. We recognize the patterns of exile and restoration, the pull of idolatry, the call of faithfulness, and the way Messiah walks the path Israel could not walk on its own.

When we open the Scriptures, we are stepping into a living narrative that still shapes us today. The same God is still at work, and the same story is still unfolding toward its promised restoration.

Reflection

  • Where have we seen repeated patterns as we read the Bible?
  • How does thinking in story form change the way we read individual verses?
  • What does it mean for us to live as characters in God’s ongoing covenant story?
